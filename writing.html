Why We Still Romanticize Indian Trains

NMIMS College Magazine · Cultural Edition 2022

A personal essay on slowness, connection, and what train travel reveals about modern India.

“There is something oddly cinematic about Indian train journeys. Maybe it’s the way conversations start with ‘Seat exchange karoge?’ and end with life stories.”

Published in the NMIMS cultural magazine, this essay explores how long-distance rail journeys have become a site of shared identity, contrasting India’s obsession with speed against the stubborn, human slowness of its trains.

There is something oddly cinematic about Indian train journeys.

Maybe it’s the way conversations start with “Seat exchange karoge?” and end with life stories. Maybe it’s the uncle who insists you try homemade theplas at 6 a.m. Or maybe it’s just the fact that nowhere else are you forced into slow living quite like this.

For a country obsessed with speed and growth, trains remain stubbornly unhurried. Flights get you there faster. Highways look shinier. But trains? Trains make you feel something.

You step onto the train. It’s chaos. There are announcements you can hardly hear, porters pushing through the crowds, and chai sellers balancing steel kettles. Once the train starts moving, things calm down. The city starts to fade as a cold wind blows on your face. The noise softens. And suddenly, you’re watching fields blur into sunsets you didn’t plan to notice.

Every coach is its own ecosystem. There’s always: the over-prepared family with four suitcases for a two-day trip, the engineering student solving aptitude questions five minutes before a campus placement, the auntie who asks your “good name” and somehow reaches your career plans within three questions.

Strangers share food. People debate politics. Someone is playing music loudly. For some reason, people just deal with it and try to get along. Music is still playing loudly, but there is this understanding that we all have to live together.

Indian trains are not comfortable in the luxury sense. They are comfortable in the familiarity sense. They are childhood summer vacations. They are long-distance friendships. They say goodbye with tears on platforms. Arrive in a daze at dawn.

In a world chasing speed, train trips show us that it’s ok to slow down sometimes. Like sipping tea from a paper cup and sitting by the window — a seat you really wanted.

Maybe that’s why we romanticize them. Because somewhere between departure and arrival, we feel a little more human.

Published in the NMIMS College Magazine, Cultural Edition 2022.

How to Interpret Heat Alerts for Public Health Planning

Illinois Local Government · Public Health Department

A practical guide translating NWS extreme heat warnings for non-technical public health staff across Illinois.

Written for local health department staff without a technical background, this guide walks through a five-step framework for reading heat alerts, identifying vulnerable populations, and coordinating response, demonstrating the ability to write clearly about technical topics for specialist-adjacent audiences.

Purpose: This guide explains how to interpret extreme heat warnings and use them to support public health planning.

1. Identify the type of alert. Start by noting whether it is a heat advisory, excessive heat watch, or excessive heat warning. Each indicates a different level of intensity and urgency.

2. Look at the time period. Check the start and end dates. Longer events may require sustained public health actions, while shorter alerts may require immediate outreach.

3. Understand key heat indicators. Pay attention to heat index values and expected daytime and nighttime temperatures. High nighttime temperatures are especially important, as they limit the body’s ability to recover from daytime heat exposure.

4. Consider population vulnerability. Older adults, outdoor workers, people with chronic health conditions, and households without reliable cooling are generally at greatest risk.

5. Calculate potential health impacts. Extreme heat can increase the likelihood of heat exhaustion, heat stroke, emergency department admissions, and worsening of existing heart or lung conditions.

Using alerts for planning: Depending on the intensity and duration of the alert, public health departments may consider sharing heat safety guidance, coordinating with local agencies to open or extend cooling centers, increasing illness monitoring, and supporting outreach to high-risk groups.

Common misinterpretations to avoid: Treating all heat alerts as equal in intensity; relying only on air temperature without considering the heat index; assuming risk is uniform across all communities.

Mumbai Awaits Its New Cultural Stage

Mid Day

A before-and-after editorial showcase: transforming a junior draft into a published piece on the Nita Mukesh Ambani Cultural Centre.

This sample demonstrates the editing process, from a serviceable but flat initial draft to a polished published article with a clear narrative voice, structural clarity, and momentum.

Initial junior draft — Vansh Jain

Mumbai is going to open a new cultural centre called the Nita Mukesh Ambani Cultural Centre in Bandra Kurla Complex. It is expected to be the biggest art and theatre in India. The centre will include different performance halls and art exhibition spaces.

The Grand Theatre will have a seating capacity of around 2,000 people and there will also be smaller studio theatres for other types of performances. There is also an Art House which will host exhibitions and installations. The idea is to bring international and Indian art together under one roof.

There are going to be many shows lined up for the opening season that include musicals, dance performances and art exhibitions. Many people are excited because this could make Mumbai come on the global cultural map.

Published version — Yashvi Bhatt, Lead Editor

Mumbai is about to gain more than a new building, more than a large auditorium, it is about to gain a stage ambitious enough to match its cultural appetite.

The Nita Mukesh Ambani Cultural Centre, all set to open on the 31st of March, in Bandra Kurla Complex, promises to reshape how the city experiences art. For decades, the heart of Bollywood has created world class talent in theatre, dance, cinema, and design. What has held it back is a space designed to hold that ambition at scale.

The numbers alone signal grandeur. A 2,000 seat Grand Theatre built for large format productions, intimate studio venues for experimental work, and a multi level Art House curated to international museum standards.

What makes this centre exciting is the programming already taking shape. Broadway-style musicals are expected to share space with Indian classical dance. Immersive art installations will sit alongside culturally rich textile exhibitions rooted in tradition. It is less about competition and more about coexistence.

This will not just be another cultural venue. It will become part of Mumbai’s rhythm, a place where art is not occasional, but constant.
